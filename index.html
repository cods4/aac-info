Unlocking Your Child's Voice

Welcome. This guide is designed to help you, as a parent of a young autistic child, navigate the world of Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC). The goal is to demystify the options and empower you with clear, practical information to support your son's communication journey.

üí¨ What is AAC?

AAC includes all the ways someone communicates besides talking. It's a set of tools and strategies to help your son express his thoughts, needs, and feelings when speech is difficult. This can range from simple gestures and picture boards to high-tech apps on a tablet. The goal is to give your son a reliable way to communicate in every situation.

üí° Myth vs. Fact

Myth: Using AAC will stop my child from learning to speak.
Fact: This is a common fear, but it's unfounded. Research and clinical experience consistently show that AAC does not stop speech development. In many cases, it actually helps! By reducing frustration and providing a consistent language model, AAC can be a stepping stone *towards* speech for children who have the potential to speak.

How to Succeed with AAC

Choosing a tool is just the first step. Success comes from how you and your support team integrate it into your son's daily life. This section outlines the key steps in the journey and the most powerful strategies you can use at home.

Your Journey: Getting Started with AAC

1

Consult a Speech-Language Pathologist (SLP)

This is the most critical first step. Find an SLP with specific expertise in AAC for young autistic children. They will be your guide through this entire process.

2

Comprehensive Assessment

The SLP will evaluate your son's unique strengths, challenges, and communication needs to determine the best type of AAC system to start with. This is called "feature matching."

3

Trial Different Systems

Don't commit to a system without trying it first! The SLP will help you trial different apps or devices to see what "clicks" for your son and what feels manageable for your family.

4

Family Training & Implementation

Once a system is chosen, the real work begins. The SLP will train you and your family on how to use it, model language, and create communication opportunities at home. Your involvement is key!

Your Most Powerful Strategy: Modeling

If you remember one thing, let it be this: you need to use your son's AAC system when you talk to him. This is called modeling or Aided Language Input.


Just like children learn to speak by hearing words used around them constantly, your son will learn his AAC system by seeing you use it. When you say, "Do you want to play?", you should also press the 'PLAY' button on his device. This shows him how the symbols connect to spoken words and real actions. It's the single most important thing you can do to help him learn and feel motivated to communicate.

Proloquo vs. Proloquo2Go: What's the Difference?

You might encounter both "Proloquo" and "Proloquo2Go" in your research. It's helpful to understand they refer to different products from the same company, AssistiveWare, designed for different platforms and eras.

Proloquo (Legacy Software)

Platform: Primarily Mac OS X (Apple desktop and laptop computers).

History: Proloquo was one of the earlier AAC software solutions developed by AssistiveWare. It was designed to provide text-to-speech capabilities and symbol-based communication on Apple computers.

Status: Proloquo for Mac OS X is generally considered legacy software. While it was innovative for its time, the focus of development and the broader AAC community has largely shifted to mobile platforms.

Relevance Today: You are less likely to encounter "Proloquo" as a current recommendation for a young child starting with AAC. Most modern AAC interventions leverage the portability and interactivity of tablets.

Proloquo2Go (Current App)

Platform: iOS (iPad, iPhone, iPod touch).

History: With the rise of the iPhone and iPad, AssistiveWare developed Proloquo2Go ("P2G") specifically for these mobile devices. It quickly became one ofthe most well-known and widely adopted AAC apps.

Status: Proloquo2Go is a current, actively developed, and widely used AAC application. It receives regular updates with new features, vocabulary, and voices.

Relevance Today: When people discuss "Proloquo" in the context of modern AAC for children, they are almost certainly referring to Proloquo2Go. This is the app you would consider for your son on an iPad.

In Simple Terms:

Think of Proloquo as the older computer program, and Proloquo2Go (P2G) as the modern app for iPads and iPhones that your son would use. If an SLP or another parent mentions "Proloquo" today in the context of an AAC app for a child, they almost always mean Proloquo2Go.

Why are AAC Apps Mostly for Apple?

You're right to notice that many of the most popular AAC apps are for iPads. While this is changing, there are several historical and technical reasons for this trend. Understanding them can help clarify why you see so many recommendations for iOS devices.

üèÜEarly Lead

When the iPad was launched in 2010, it was a game-changer. Developers rushed to create AAC apps for it, establishing a strong ecosystem early on. This gave iOS a significant head start.

üõ°Ô∏èPlatform Stability

Apple's hardware and software are tightly integrated, creating a very stable and consistent environment. For developers of complex apps like AAC, this makes building and maintaining them more straightforward.

‚ôøBuilt-in Accessibility

Apple has long prioritized powerful, built-in accessibility features. A crucial one is "Guided Access," which locks the iPad into a single app. This was vital for keeping a child focused on their communication app.

üòéSocial Acceptance

Using a mainstream device like an iPad often carries less stigma than a specialized "medical" device. It's the "cool" device that other kids have, which can help a child feel more included.

üìàIs it Changing?

Yes! The Android platform has made huge strides in accessibility, and more quality AAC apps are becoming available for it. However, the widest selection of the most established apps is still found on iOS due to its long history in the field.

‚û°Ô∏èWhat This Means For You

While you'll encounter many iPad recommendations, it's worth discussing Android options with your SLP, especially if budget is a concern or your family prefers that platform.

Professional Organizations

  • ASHA (American Speech-Language-Hearing Association): Find qualified professionals and get reliable information on communication disorders.
  • ISAAC (International Society for AAC): Global organization dedicated to promoting communication for people with complex needs.

Autism & Community Support

  • Autism Speaks: Provides toolkits and resources on autism, including assistive technology.
  • PrAACtical AAC: A highly respected blog with practical tips and resources from AAC experts.

Device & Funding Support

  • State Assistive Technology (AT) Programs (USA): Find your state's program for device loans, demos, and funding info.
  • AAC Vendor Websites: Companies like Tobii Dynavox, PRC-Saltillo, and AssistiveWare have extensive support materials and community forums.

Parent & Online Groups

Search on social media platforms like Facebook for groups dedicated to specific AAC apps (e.g., "LAMP Words for Life Parents") or general AAC parent support groups. Connecting with other parents can provide invaluable advice and emotional support.
